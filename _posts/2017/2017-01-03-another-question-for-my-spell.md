Another question for my spell book project. What does everyone think about the old D&amp;D spells and how they kind of crossed classes at time? Like how both Wizards and Clerics could cast Hold Person, Summon Monster, or Animate Dead? Would you want to have those spells show up in both the Arcane and Divine spell compendiums I&#39;m working on? Let me know in the poll!<br /><br />

  <h4><img src='{{site.baseurl}}//images/avatars/102595580176380683252_photo.jpg'> Jeremy Strandberg</h4>
      <p><cite>2017-01-03 15:12:32 (edited: 2017-01-03 15:12:32)</cite></p>
        <p>Depends entirely on the rest of the context.  Assuming you&#39;re using the core DW Cleric and Wizard classes as written, then I&#39;d keep them separate (yeah, yeah, <i>Light</i> is on both lists, but c&#39;mon, it&#39;s <b><i>Light</i></b>).  <br /><br />Reason 1: It helps thematically differentiate between them.<br /><br />Reason 2: It&#39;s a good design constraint, to have to make any given spell fit <i>clearly</i> under &quot;Cleric&quot; or &quot;Wizard.&quot;<br /><br />Reason 3:  If the spell lists overlap, the human wizard and cleric moves lose some of their appeal, and the become messier.  (e.g. player accidentally chooses a wizard spell that was already a cleric spell!)<br /><br />...<br /><br />Now, if you&#39;re tweaking the underlying classes, then I think what <span class="proflinkWrapper"><span class="proflinkPrefix">+</span><a class="proflink" href="https://plus.google.com/103667855585775066713" oid="103667855585775066713">Aaron Griffin</a></span> is describing has legs: cleric vs. wizard could have less to do with the types of spells they cast and more to do with their focus on spells or divine smiting.<br /><br />Like, for a different fantasy hack I was working on, I divided spells into Spheres (blood, chaos, death, fire, restoration, etc.... something like 15 of them, each with a cantrip and 4-6 additional spells).  Wizards got to pick 2 or 3 starting moves, each of which granted access to 1 - 3 spheres, as well as a thematic special ability.  <br /><br />So, like, if they choose, <b>Dark Arts</b>, the got access to the <i>darkness</i> and <i>death</i> spheres and could also see ghosts and shades of the dead, and the impressions left by death.   If they chose <b>Occult Order</b> they got to pick two spheres of their choice that defined they&#39;re order&#39;s magic, and got influence and access to the order&#39;s resources.  If they chose <b>Sanctified</b> they got <i>protection</i> and <i>restoration</i> and <i>spirit</i> and the ability to ask &quot;what here is touched by Evil?&quot;<br /><br />Similarly, there was Templar playbook that had a bunch of moves about smiting enemies of the faith, and a few available moves that granted access to spheres.</p>
</div>

And so is the wizard peering into their crystal ball (or whatever).<br /><br />I think what you should do is consider the <i>style</i> of each spell and which class it fits.  Or decide which class you <i>want</i> a spell to go with, and make sure it&#39;s description and function fits that class.<br /><br />It&#39;s a subtle distinction about which type of spell fits where, but in my mind it breaks down to:<br /><br /><b>Wizards</b> tamper with forces best left alone. They bind, twist, harness, exploit, defy, bargain. They fling open Death&#39;s Black Gates to blast their foes with a withering black wind, conjure up a dead man&#39;s shade by leveraging their link to a once-beloved possession, or send their senses off into the void to spy upon their foes. <br /><br /><b>Clerics</b> wield the authority and power of their deity. They command, beseech, abjure, and censure. The invoke spirits or entities that respect their god. The wield a fraction of their god&#39;s domain, or their command over the fundamental forces of the cosmos. They smite their god&#39;s foes. They bless or curse in their god&#39;s name!  <br /><br />Wizards are hackers. Clerics are representatives. Their spells should reflect that.</p>
</div>
